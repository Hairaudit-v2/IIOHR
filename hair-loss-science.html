Training Pathways

Hair loss science and trichology

Advanced education in hair-loss biology, trichology, and diagnosis — so technique is informed by deeper understanding. Theory and practice integrated at every stage of the pathway.

Why theory matters for surgeons

Technique without understanding has a ceiling. IIOHR integrates advanced theory so your surgical decisions are science-informed.

Hair restoration is not only a technical procedure — it involves diagnosis, planning, and judgment that depend on a solid grasp of hair-loss biology, patient assessment, and trichology. Our curriculum covers the science behind pattern loss, donor assessment, and treatment planning so that when you perform FUE or advise patients, you are drawing on a clear conceptual foundation. That integration of practical and theoretical excellence is what differentiates surgeon development from short-format technique courses.

Curriculum themes

Structured education in the science that underlies hair restoration practice.

  • Hair-loss biology and pattern recognition
  • Donor assessment and planning
  • Trichology and scalp assessment
  • Diagnosis and treatment planning
  • Integration of theory with surgical technique

Follicle biology and anatomy

Understanding the hair follicle — structure, growth cycle, and how biology informs surgical planning.

The curriculum builds from a clear foundation in follicle anatomy and the biology of hair growth. Cross-sectional and longitudinal understanding of the follicle supports donor assessment, extraction planning, and handling — so technique is grounded in science.

Hormones and androgens

The role of DHT, testosterone, and androgen sensitivity in pattern hair loss.

Androgenetic alopecia is driven by hormonal and genetic factors. The curriculum covers the science of DHT, receptor sensitivity, and how this informs diagnosis and treatment planning — so surgeons understand why certain patterns emerge and how to assess progression.

Hair cycle phases

Anagen, catagen, telogen — and why the cycle matters for surgery and outcomes.

Hair grows in distinct phases. Understanding the cycle supports timing, planning, and expectation-setting. The curriculum integrates this so that surgical and non-surgical decisions are aligned with biology.

Genetics and predisposition

How genetic factors influence pattern, progression, and patient counselling.

Family history and genetic predisposition inform both diagnosis and long-term planning. The pathway covers how to incorporate genetics into assessment and patient communication — without overstating or oversimplifying.

Male pattern hair loss

Presentation, staging, and surgical considerations in male androgenetic alopecia.

Norwood and related classifications, donor assessment, and planning for male pattern loss are core to the curriculum. Theory and practice are linked so that staging informs technique and expectation management.

Female presentation and patient perspective

How hair loss presents in women and why assessment and counselling differ.

Female pattern loss and other presentations require distinct assessment and communication. The curriculum covers presentation, differential diagnosis, and patient-centred counselling so that surgical and non-surgical options are discussed appropriately.

How it fits the full pathway

Hair loss science and trichology run through the IIOHR pathway — from foundation through to refinement.

Theory is introduced before and alongside hands-on training, so you build both knowledge and skill in parallel. As you progress to live-patient work and then to post-training auditing, the same concepts support your case review and improvement. We do not treat theory as a one-off module: it is part of structured progression and long-term support.

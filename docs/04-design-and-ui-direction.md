# IIOHR Website Strategy — Design & UI Direction

## Tone and Visual Direction (Summary)

The site should feel: **elite, clinical, intelligent, global, premium, disciplined, modern, evidence-led, supportive, transformational.**

Internal creative reference (not for literal use on the site): *Harvard + Mayo Clinic + flight simulator* — the seriousness of top-tier education, the credibility of a leading clinical institution, and the rigour of high-stakes simulation and measurement.

---

## Visual Direction

### Overall Vibe
- **Restraint over noise.** White space, clear hierarchy, no visual clutter.
- **Medical-education credibility.** Feels like an institute or professional body, not a course marketplace.
- **Calm authority.** Confident typography and spacing; no urgent banners or countdown timers.
- **Global and contemporary.** Imagery and references feel international and current, not parochial or dated.

### Colour
- **Primary:** Deep navy or charcoal for text and key UI — conveys seriousness and readability.
- **Secondary:** Muted, clinical accents (e.g. soft teal, steel blue, or warm grey) for links, borders, and subtle emphasis. Avoid bright “course” colours (orange, lime, purple).
- **Backgrounds:** White or very light neutral (off-white, light grey) as base; optional subtle tint for section contrast.
- **Contrast:** WCAG AA minimum; ensure body text and CTAs are clearly legible.

### Typography
- **Headings:** Strong serif or refined sans-serif; authoritative but not stiff. Examples of *direction* (not mandates): serif for “institute” gravitas, or a clean geometric sans for “modern discipline.”
- **Body:** Highly readable serif or sans-serif; 18–20px or equivalent for main copy on desktop; comfortable line height and line length.
- **Hierarchy:** Clear H1 → H2 → H3; one clear hero headline per page. No decorative font overload.

### Imagery
- **Photography:** Prefer real context where possible — theatre, hands, teaching, clinical environments — without looking like generic stock. Avoid “happy graduate” clichés and staged diversity tropes.
- **Tone of imagery:** Calm, focused, professional. No hype or salesy expressions.
- **Illustration:** If used, minimal and purposeful (e.g. pathway diagrams, simple icons for differentiators). Not cartoonish or playful.
- **Video:** If used, short and substantive (e.g. faculty, environment, process). No autoplay with sound.

### Motion and Interactivity
- **Subtle and purposeful.** Light scroll or hover effects only where they aid clarity (e.g. pathway steps, cards). No parallax overload or decorative animation.
- **Performance:** Fast load; no heavy animation that blocks content or feels gimmicky.

## Recommended UI Direction for a Premium Medical-Education Brand

### Layout
- **Generous whitespace.** Sections breathe; content is grouped in clear blocks.
- **Grid:** Consistent, simple grid (e.g. 12-column); alignment is strict. No chaotic or “creative” breaks unless they serve hierarchy.
- **Sections:** One main idea per section; clear section titles. Homepage sections as defined in Part 3.

### Navigation
- **Clear and minimal.** Primary nav: Home, About, Training Pathways (with dropdown), Standards, For Clinics, Faculty, Outcomes. Apply / Enquire as a distinct, visible button (e.g. outline or solid accent).
- **Sticky header:** Optional; if used, keep it slim and unobtrusive. Apply remains visible.
- **Footer:** Full sitemap or key links; optional short tagline; legal links.

### Components
- **Buttons:** One primary style (Apply / Enquire), one secondary (e.g. outline or text). Consistent size and padding; no pill-shaped or neon styles that suggest “cheap course.”
- **Cards:** If used for pathway steps, differentiators, or faculty — clean borders or subtle shadows; clear typography; no decorative gradients or clip-art.
- **Forms:** Apply / Enquire form: clear labels, logical order, optional short progress or grouping. No unnecessary fields. Thank-you or confirmation state defined.
- **CTAs:** Same language as strategy: “Apply / Enquire,” “See the pathway,” “Explore [topic].” No “Buy now,” “Enrol today,” or “Get certified fast.”

### Content Presentation
- **Scannable.** Short paragraphs; bullets where they aid clarity. Key messages in Tier 1–2 (see Part 1) visible in headings or lead sentences.
- **No hype blocks.** Avoid “World-leading,” “#1,” “Revolutionary” unless backed by a clear, credible claim. Prefer “pathway,” “measured improvement,” “structured support.”
- **Evidence-led tone.** Where we mention auditing or outcomes, use “measured,” “audit-led,” “data-informed” — not “guaranteed” or “proven results.”

### Technical and UX
- **Mobile-first or responsive.** Readable and usable on small screens; nav collapses cleanly; CTAs remain accessible.
- **Accessibility:** Semantic HTML, focus states, alt text, colour contrast as above. Aim for WCAG 2.1 AA where feasible.
- **Performance:** Optimise images and scripts; avoid blocking render. Fast load supports “premium” perception.

### What to Avoid
- **Cheap course cues:** Countdown timers, “Only X spots left,” badges, garish colours, crammed layouts.
- **Generic academy look:** Template-style “course cards,” star ratings, pricing tables front and centre (if pricing is shown, it’s in context of Apply/Enquire).
- **Overclaim in design:** No “master” or “certified” badges that imply guaranteed outcome; no regulatory-looking seals unless legally accurate.
- **Clutter:** No social proof walls, pop-ups, or aggressive sign-up forms. One clear Apply/Enquire path.

## Brand Feel Checklist (Quick Reference)

| Attribute | Do | Don’t |
|-----------|----|-------|
| **Elite** | Confident, high bar, selective language | Arrogant or exclusive in a negative way |
| **Clinical** | Precise, clean, medical-education tone | Cold or impersonal |
| **Intelligent** | Assume educated audience; use correct terms | Jargon for its own sake |
| **Premium** | Spacious, restrained, considered UI | Flashy or salesy |
| **Evidence-led** | Auditing, data, improvement, pathway | Guarantees, “#1,” unbacked claims |
| **Supportive** | Mentoring, pathway, “we accompany” | Overpromise or hype |
